Post-Sales Negotiation Playbook

Tactical guide for handling pricing conversations, renewals, expansions, and every negotiation moment in between.

1

Intel Gathering

Before you negotiate anything, you need information. The person with the most information almost always wins.

Earn the Right to Have a Call

Before you schedule any call, ask yourself: would I spend 30 minutes on this if I were them? If you can't articulate what you're bringing to the table (a recommendation, an insight, a new feature tied to an outcome they care about) the call isn't strong enough. Every meeting should make them feel like they're talking to a consultant who's helping them hit their personal goals, not a vendor checking a box.

Say This

"I've been digging into your account and have a few recommendations I want to walk you through- one around how your team is using sequences that I think could meaningfully move your reply rates, and another on a new feature we shipped that maps directly to the pipeline target you mentioned last quarter. Want to grab 30 minutes this week?"

Don't Say This

"Hey, wanted to schedule our regular check-in." "Just wanted to touch base." "It's been a while since we connected."- if the only reason for the call is that it's been a while, you haven't earned it. Generic check-ins signal that you have nothing valuable to say.

Why It Works

When every meeting delivers real value, you become the person they want to talk to, not the person they dodge. That positioning as a consultant rather than a vendor compounds over time and makes every future conversation (including negotiation) easier. The best way to build trust is to provide value.

Key Rule

Before you send any meeting invite, write down exactly what you're bringing. If your list is empty or amounts to "status update," cancel it. Prep a recommendation, pull a metric, connect a feature to their goal. If you can't think of why it's worth 30 minutes of their day, it's a waste of yours as well.

How to Find Out Who Needs to Be Looped In

Asking "who's the decision maker?" puts people on guard and feels like you're trying to go around them. Ask about the procurement process instead. It's neutral, professional, and gets you the same information: decision makers, budget holders, legal, procurement timelines.

Say This

"Just so we can plan accordingly on our end- what does your procurement process typically look like for something like this?"

Don't Say This

"Who's the decision maker on this?" / "Who controls the budget?" / "Do you have authority to sign?"

Why It Works

You come across as organized and process-oriented, not as someone trying to maneuver. They usually volunteer every stakeholder, timeline, and approval gate without realizing they've given you the full decision map.

Key Rule

Ask this in the first or second conversation- never wait until pricing is on the table. By then, surprises about who else needs to approve will stall your deal.

Mirroring

Repeat the last one to three critical words someone just said, then go silent. People feel compelled to elaborate. It's the simplest, most effective information-extraction technique that exists.

Say This

Customer: "We're looking at potentially scaling back some of our tools."
You: "Scaling back some of your tools?"
Customer: "Yeah, our CFO is pushing everyone to cut 15% across the board. It's not really about Unify specifically, it's more of a blanket mandate..."

You just learned it's a CFO-driven mandate, the target is 15%, and it's not specific to your tool. All from three words and a pause.

Don't Say This

Don't respond to their statement with your own agenda. "Scaling back? Well let me tell you about all the value we've delivered..." kills the moment. Mirror first, listen, then respond.

Why It Works

Mirroring signals similarity- "trust me, we're alike." It triggers a deep biological instinct to elaborate. People start offering up information without realizing it.

Key Rule

After you mirror, shut up. The silence is uncomfortable- that's the point. If you talk after mirroring, you've killed it.

Labeling

Identify what someone is feeling, turn it into words, and say it back to them. When someone feels heard, they become less defensive and more willing to share real information. Labels always start with "It sounds like..." or "It seems like..."- never "I think you..." because that makes it about you.

Say This

"It sounds like there's a lot of internal pressure on this renewal that goes beyond just the price."

"It seems like you've been dealing with a lot of competing priorities and this has added more complexity than expected."

Don't Say This

"I think you're frustrated." / "I understand how you feel."- both of these make it about you and feel patronizing. "It sounds like..." puts it on them to confirm or correct.

Why It Works

When you label correctly, you'll hear "That's right" — the two most powerful words in any negotiation. "That's right" means they feel understood. "You're right" means they want you to shut up- totally different.

Key Rule

After you label, pause. Let them react. If you nailed it, they'll open up. If you were off, they'll correct you- which is also valuable information.

The Dig-In vs. Move-On Decision

Every time the customer speaks, you're at a fork: dig in on what they just said, or change topics. Most reps operate on autopilot. Great reps consciously decide every single time: "Is staying on this topic the best use of time to close this deal?" Default to digging in.

Say This (Digging In)

Customer: "Onboarding times for new hires is a big topic for us."
You: "What's making this something that's high on your list right now? Is this something leadership is pushing for, or something you've identified as a big opportunity?"

Don't Say This (Autopilot response)

"Great, yeah onboarding is important. So anyway, next thing I wanted to cover..."- you just blew past the most important thing the customer said on the call.

Why It Works

Topics worth going deeper: the problem, why the problem matters, what their boss thinks, how they'll justify the spend, and economics. Topics to deflect: technical rabbit holes early in the call, integrations before value is established. Good heuristic: If it's high-level, dig in. If it's in the weeds, move on.

Key Rule

If they bring up something in the first 5 minutes that sounds like a rabbit hole (integrations, security, compliance), acknowledge it and park it: "We'll absolutely go deep on that- let me make a note so we don't miss it. First, I'd love to understand..."

The Accusation Audit

Before any conversation where you're expecting resistance (a price increase, a renewal push, bad news) list out every negative thing the customer could possibly think about you, then say it first. This disarms them before they can dig in.

Say This

"Before we dig in, I want to acknowledge something. You're probably thinking we're about to come in and ask for more money, and that we should just be grateful you renewed at all given how tight budgets are. And you'd be right to think that. I want to make sure this conversation feels fair."

Don't Say This

Don't pretend tough conversations are easy. "So excited to talk about the renewal!" when you know they're about to push back on price sets a fake tone from the start.

Why It Works

By saying the worst version of what they're thinking, you neutralize it. They usually respond with something softer: "No, it's not that bad, I just want to understand the value." You've moved from adversarial to collaborative in one sentence.

Key Rule

Go further than feels comfortable. If your accusation audit is mild, it won't land. The more specific and unflinching it is, the more disarming it becomes.

Surfacing What Happens in the Room You're Not In

Your champion will tell you what they think. They won't volunteer what their boss, the CFO, or procurement will object to- unless you ask. This question lets them put objections on the table without owning them personally. You should always be thinking: how is my champion going to sell this internally when I'm not in the room?

Say This

"You obviously know [boss name] better than I do. If you floated this to her right now, what do you think her initial reaction would be? Thinking about that might give us ideas for topics we should spend extra time on."

Don't Say This

"Will your boss be okay with this?"- too vague, and it positions you as hoping for the best instead of building the case.

Why It Works

This surfaces the real objections you'll face two levels up, and gives you time to arm your champion with materials that address them before they ever walk into that room.

Key Rule

When they tell you what the boss will push back on, build a one-pager or data summary that directly addresses it. Send it to your champion 48 hours before their internal meeting. Don't offer to help, just build it and send it.

2

Discovery

Understand what they need, what success looks like, and what will make the renewal an easy yes — before you ever talk numbers.

Opening the Renewal Conversation

One question tells you exactly how to structure the entire rest of the conversation. Ask it early, not at the end. The answer reveals their decision criteria, internal pressures, and what evidence they need to bring upstairs.

Say This

"Before I jump into anything- if we're thinking about Year 2 together, what would it take for this to be an easy yes on your end? What does [boss name / leadership] need to see to feel good about it?"

Don't Say This

Don't wait until the end of a 45-minute call to ask this. By then the customer has decided whether you've earned the renewal based on whatever they chose to focus on. You want to control the frame from the start.

Why It Works

It hands them the pen. They tell you exactly how to win, in their own language, and it feels collaborative instead of salesy. Whatever they say becomes the structure for every meeting from here until signature.

Key Rule

Write down their answer verbatim. Use their exact words back to them in the value review, the proposal, and the business case. When they hear their own language reflected back, it feels like you built this for them- because you did.

Calibrated Questions

Questions that start with "How" or "What" and have no fixed answer. They buy you time, make the other side feel in control, and force them to solve your problems, all without you revealing your constraints.

Say This

"How am I supposed to do that?" (when they ask for something unreasonable)
"What does a successful outcome look like for you?"
"How would you like me to proceed?"
"What's the biggest challenge with this right now?"
"How does this fit with what your team is trying to accomplish?"
"What happens if you do nothing?"

Don't Say This

Avoid closed questions that give them a binary out: "Does that work for you?" / "Can you do $X?" — these invite "no" without any problem-solving.

Why It Works

These questions shift the mental burden. The conversation frame moves from "How will I get my deal done?" to "How will THEY get my deal done?" — and they do it willingly because the question feels collaborative.

Key Rule

"How am I supposed to do that?" is the single most powerful calibrated question. Use it when they demand something unreasonable. It's not aggressive, it's genuinely asking them to help you solve the problem.

Getting to "No" to Get to "Yes"

"Yes" is a commitment people protect themselves from. "No" feels safe- it gives them control. Design your questions so the other side says "No" to arrive at the answer you want.

Say This

Instead of "Do you have a few minutes to talk?"- "Is now a bad time?" ("No" = "It's fine, go ahead.")

In a renewal: "Is Year 2 just not on the table right now?" ("No" = "We haven't given up"- they've committed to continuing.)

Don't Say This

"Just checking in!" / "Wanted to circle back!"- these are easy to ignore because they don't force a reaction. A question designed for "No" forces engagement.

Why It Works

Saying "no" makes people feel safe and in control. Once they feel safe, they open up. A "no" answer to a well-designed question often means "yes" to what you actually want.

Key Rule

This works especially well in email. Messages designed for "No" get dramatically faster replies than messages asking for "Yes."

Setting the Right Success Metrics from Day One

Most teams only track tactical metrics, i.e. emails sent, open rates, mailbox health. If the only number you can show at renewal is "we sent 50K emails," you've lost. You need to capture metrics at three levels: what the executive team cares about (pipeline generated, revenue influenced, cost per meeting), what the ops team cares about (signal conversion rates, SDR productivity), and what we manage day-to-day (deliverability, mailbox utilization, credits consumed).

Say This (at kickoff)

"I don't want to just track whether we're hitting deliverability benchmarks. What I want to understand is: what numbers does your VP or CRO look at every quarter? If we do our job perfectly, what moves? Let's set those as our real goals so when we come back in 90 days, we're talking about pipeline and revenue, not just emails."

Don't Say This

Don't settle for "roll out to X reps by Y date" as a success metric. That's an implementation milestone, not a business outcome. Push for the numbers that matter to the person who signs the check.

Why It Works

Both the renewal value story and any expansion pitch start with "here's the impact you've seen." If you didn't capture meaningful goals at launch, you'll have nothing to anchor to. You'll be stuck saying "We've been nice and responsive" instead of "You've generated $2M in pipeline."

Key Rule

Capture these in writing at the kickoff meeting. Get both sides to agree on what you'll track and report against. The first QBR an executive attends should show real business impact- if it doesn't, it'll be the last QBR they attend.

3

Value Anchoring

Before any pricing conversation, cement the value you've delivered. The customer who feels ROI-rich before hearing a number negotiates completely differently.

Lead with ROI Before Any Pricing Conversation

Research proves that reinforcing the value you've delivered (documenting results, reminding them of their selection process, highlighting switching risk) beats provocative "new feature" messaging by +13% in renewal intention. You ARE the status quo. Reinforce it, don't disrupt it.

Say This

"Before we talk about anything commercial- you've generated [$X] in pipeline through Unify at a cost of [$Y] for the year. That's [Z]x ROI. The question isn't whether [$Y] is expensive, it's whether you can afford to lose what's generating [$X] in pipeline."

Don't Say This

Don't jump into pricing, packaging, or contract terms before the value conversation. If they haven't internalized the ROI, every number feels expensive.

Why It Works

Present your attribution framework first: "We measure all pipeline influenced. Does that match how you measure internally?" If they use a different methodology, adapt to theirs. Owning the attribution narrative is half the battle.

Key Rule

Always quantify in their language. Pipeline generated, meetings booked, cost per meeting, deals influenced- not emails sent or open rates. Speak the language their leadership speaks.

Making the Cost of Switching Tangible

People place at least 2x as much value on avoiding a loss as gaining something new. When you surface switching costs, you activate loss aversion- the most powerful motivator in decision-making. Be specific about what they'd actually have to rebuild.

Say This

"I want to be transparent about something. If you switch, here's what the transition actually looks like: your mailboxes took three weeks to warm up- you'd be starting that from scratch. That's three weeks of zero outbound volume. You're running [X] plays right now generating pipeline daily- all of that stops during migration. Your CRM sync, the routing rules your team relies on- all of that has to be rebuilt. And we've spent months learning your business, your ICP, what messaging works. A new vendor starts at zero. The realistic timeline to get back to where you are today is 3-6 months, if you even get back here at all."

Don't Say This

Don't be vague: "Switching would be hard." Make it specific. Hard numbers, real timelines, named features. The more concrete, the more real the risk feels.

Why It Works

You're not fearmongering, you're stating facts. Mailboxes cost ~$25/month each and take 3 weeks to warm. Credits are $0.10 each. Onboarding takes 6 weeks. These are real costs in time and money. Your customer's Status Quo Bias will amplify the rest.

Key Rule

Mention switching risk, don't hammer it. Be factual and empathetic. The risk of not mentioning it is far greater than the risk of planting a seed.

The Hero Story

Aggregate numbers are forgettable. One specific deal walked through step-by-step (signal detected, outreach sent, meeting booked, deal created)- is unforgettable. Always have at least one ready for every customer conversation.

Say This

"Let me show you one deal. On March 12th, a web visitor from [Company] hit your pricing page. Unify detected the signal, auto-enrolled the right contact into your High Intent sequence, and your SDR got a call task with full context. They connected on the first call. That deal is now in Stage 3 at $85K. Without Unify, that visitor bounces and nobody ever knows they were there."

Don't Say This

"We influenced $2M in pipeline."- too abstract. The hero story makes it real. Show one deal they recognize, not a dashboard number.

Why It Works

The customer recognizes the deal and validates it. Abstract ROI becomes personal. One hero story often does more to secure a renewal than an entire QBR deck of charts.

Key Rule

Prep this before every call. Pull one named deal from their pipeline that Unify touched. Walk through the full journey: signal → sequence → engagement → meeting → deal. Make them see it.

"You" Language

Research shows "you-phrased" messages make customers +21% more likely to feel responsible for solving the problem, +13% more likely to take action, and +9% more likely to feel the issue matters to their org. The customer is the hero. You are the guide.

Say This

"You've reduced your pipeline gap by 40%." / "You're generating 15 meetings a month that didn't exist before."

Don't Say This

"Our solution reduced your pipeline gap by 40%." / "Through our partnership, we generated 15 meetings."- makes it about you, not them.

Why It Works

People recall information at higher rates when it's about themselves and engage more when they see themselves as the protagonist. They're more likely to fight to protect something they built than something a vendor provided.

Key Rule

Audit your QBR deck, your emails, your talk tracks. Replace every "we" with "you" where possible. It's a small change that shifts how the entire relationship feels.

Reminding Them of Their Original Decision

People don't like admitting they made bad decisions. Reminding them how much work went into choosing you (the evaluation, the pilot, the executive buy-in) reinforces that they did their due diligence. It also reminds them how painful that process was. Why repeat it?

Say This

"When your team evaluated tools 18 months ago, you looked at four or five options, ran a pilot, got buy-in from leadership, and made a really deliberate choice. That took what- two, three months? The work you've done since then has built on that foundation, and I want to make sure we protect that progress."

Don't Say This

Don't assume they remember. If the people in the room now weren't the original buyers, this context is even more powerful- they probably don't know how thorough the evaluation was.

Why It Works

Reminding them of their original decision reinforces preference stability- one of four forces behind status quo bias. They made a decision, invested time and political capital, and it's working. Why risk all of that?

Key Rule

If you don't know the history, just ask: "I wasn't here when you first chose us- can you tell me a bit about why you did?" Customers often talk themselves into renewing on the spot because they remind themselves of the value.

Sell the Outcome, Not the Workflow

The person using your product day-to-day cares about workflows- how it fits into their routine, how many clicks, how it integrates). But the person signing the check cares about outcomes- pipeline generated, revenue influenced, time saved, cost reduced. When you pitch the workflow to the decision maker, you've lost. When you pitch the outcome to the practitioner, you've confused them. Match your message to the altitude of the audience.

Say This

To the decision maker: "Your team is generating 40% more qualified pipeline than they were before Unify. We can walk through how if you'd like, but the result is what matters."

To the practitioner: "Let me show you exactly how this fits into your daily workflow so you can see how little changes on your end."

Don't Say This

Don't walk a VP through a step-by-step product demo when they need a one-slide summary of business impact. And don't pitch ROI numbers to the SDR manager when they want to know how sequences actually work.

Why It Works

Decision makers operate at the outcome level- they're deciding whether results justify cost. If you force them to translate workflows into outcomes in their head, most won't bother. They'll just say "let me think about it," which means no. Give them the conclusion, not the evidence trail.

Key Rule

Before every meeting, identify who in the room is a workflow buyer and who is an outcome buyer. Prepare different talk tracks for each. One slide for the exec, one walkthrough for the practitioner. Never mix altitudes.

Arm Your Champion with 5 Key Questions

Your champion wants to go to bat for you internally, but they don't know how. Most deals stall not because the champion said no, but because they walked into an internal meeting unprepared and got picked apart. Your job is to make your champion look like the smartest person in the room. Ask these five questions, then build them the materials they need to win.

Say This

"I want to make sure you're set up to sell this internally. Can we walk through five quick things?

1. When you bring this up, what's the first question [boss name] is going to ask?
2. Who else in the room will have an opinion, and what will they care about?
3. What's the one objection you're most worried about?
4. If they ask 'why now', what would you say?
5. What would make you feel bulletproof walking in there?"

Don't Say This

"Let me know if you need anything for the internal meeting." This puts the burden on them to figure out what they need. They won't ask- they'll just wing it. And winging it is how deals die in rooms you're not in.

Why It Works

You can't be in every internal meeting, but you can control the narrative by arming your champion. These five questions surface the real objections, identify the real stakeholders, and give you the exact materials to build. When your champion walks in prepared, they don't just advocate- they close.

Key Rule

After this conversation, build a one-pager or email summary that directly addresses every concern they raised. Send it 48 hours before their internal meeting, not as an offer to help, but as a done deliverable. "Here's something I put together that might be useful" is 10x more powerful than "let me know if you need support."

Context Statements

Before you show any feature, capability, or result- set it up with a context statement. The formula is: Outcome they want → Challenge standing in the way → How this solves it. Without context, features are just features. With context, every feature becomes a direct answer to a problem they've already told you about.

Say This

"You mentioned your team needs to hit 150 qualified meetings this quarter [outcome], but your reps are spending 3 hours a day on manual prospecting [challenge]. This is where our automation layer comes in- it handles the sequencing so your reps can focus purely on the conversations that convert [solution]."

Don't Say This

"Let me show you our automation feature. So here you can set up sequences, add steps, configure timing..."- Feature first walkthroughs lose people. Without the context of why this feature matters to them specifically, it's just a product tour.

Why It Works

Context statements do three things at once: they prove you were listening (you're referencing their goals), they make the feature feel personally relevant (not generic), and they frame every capability as a solution to their specific problem. The customer stops evaluating features and starts seeing answers.

Key Rule

Never show a feature without a context statement. Before every demo, QBR, or review, write down the Outcome → Challenge → Solution for each thing you plan to show. If you can't connect a feature to something the customer told you they care about, cut it from the agenda.

4

Price Negotiation

When pricing comes up, never blink first. These tactics help you hold value, extract their real expectations, and protect deal economics.

Making Them Reveal Their Number First

When a customer says "can you do anything on price?", the instinct is to offer 10-20% off. Don't. You've just discounted against yourself with zero information. Make them tell you what they're expecting. Most of the time, their number is better than what you would have offered.

Say This

Step 1: "I appreciate you being direct. What kind of number were you expecting?"

Step 2 (if they won't say): "Help me understand where we are- am I way off? Like, are we 50% higher than what you were thinking?"

By naming a ridiculous number (50%), you anchor them to an extreme they'd never ask for. They'll correct you: "Oh no, nothing like that- we were thinking more like 15-20%." Now you know their range.

Don't Say This

"Can you do anything on price?" → "Sure, I can do 20% off." You just gave away 20% when they might have been happy with 10%. Or 5%.

Why It Works

The first person to name a number sets the anchor. If you name a discount first, you're anchoring against yourself. If they name their expectation first, you negotiate from their floor, not your ceiling.

Key Rule

Never, ever offer a discount that wasn't asked for. If they haven't pushed on price, don't introduce the idea that price is negotiable.

Anchoring High, Then Offering a Justified Discount

The brain anchors to the first number it hears and judges everything relative to it. This works even when people know they're being anchored. Research tested six different approaches to price increases- anchoring at a higher price and then offering a loyalty discount won across every metric.

Say This

"Based on new capabilities and what comparable tools cost in market, this would normally be about an 8% increase. But because of the partnership we've built and the timeline we're working with, I've gotten approval to hold it at 4%."

Don't Say This

"Due to rising costs, we need to increase by 4%."- the most common approach, and research shows it underperforms anchoring. Blaming external factors doesn't help, it's still their problem.

Why It Works

If you need a minimum 2% increase: don't ask for 2% (they negotiate you down). Anchor at 6% with justification, offer a loyalty discount to 4%. You've exceeded your minimum and the customer feels good about getting a deal.

Key Rule

Anchoring works on more than price. You can anchor contract length ("most customers do three years"), mailbox counts ("teams your size typically run 50 mailboxes"), and timeline ("most renewals take 3-4 weeks to finalize").

Decreasing Concessions

When you do have to make concessions, make each one progressively smaller. This signals you're approaching your limit. A series of equal-sized concessions tells them there's always more room.

Say This

If your target is $100K and they push back:
Round 1: Start at $115K.
Round 2: Move to $107K. (dropped $8K)
Round 3: Move to $102K. (dropped $5K)
Final: "$99,850- and I'll include an extra month of onboarding support." (dropped $2.15K, plus non-monetary sweetener)

Use a precise, non-round final number. $99,850 feels researched and firm. $100K feels arbitrary and negotiable.

Don't Say This

Don't make equal concessions: $115K → $110K → $105K → $100K. This pattern screams "keep pushing, there's more."

Why It Works

Each smaller concession tells the other side you're getting closer to your limit. The non-round final number + a small non-monetary item signals "I've given you everything I possibly can." They feel like they squeezed every last drop out of you.

Key Rule

Precise numbers ($47,850 not $48,000) are perceived as more researched and get outcomes closer to target. Round numbers feel arbitrary. Use precise numbers on your final offer.

Give-Get

Every concession is a strategic exchange. You never give anything without getting something back. Longer commitment for a better rate. Early signature for credits. Case study for a discount. Referral introduction for a rate lock. Nothing is free.

Say This

"I hear you on budget. Here's what I can do- if you move to a two-year agreement, I can get you a preferred rate that brings this down to where you need to be. We're trading certainty for savings, which works for both of us."

"If we can get this signed by end of month, I can include 5,000 additional credits at no cost. That gives your team runway to test the new signal plays we talked about."

Don't Say This

Don't offer free seats, credits, or extensions without getting something in return. Giving freebies when pushed teaches the customer that pushback = free stuff, every time.

Why It Works

It reframes the conversation from "I need you to lower the price" to "Let's figure out a structure that works for both of us." The customer feels like they negotiated something real. You preserved your economics.

Key Rule

Before any negotiation, have your give-get menu ready. Know exactly what you can trade (contract length, payment terms, case study, referral, early signature) and what each is worth to you.

Turning a Price Conversation into a Scoping Conversation

When someone says "the price is too high," they often haven't connected scope to need. Redirect from price to scope- show why every line item exists and what happens if you remove it.

Say This

"Before we talk numbers, I want to make sure we're scoping this right. Based on what we've discussed, you need [X] mailboxes for the team- each one takes three weeks to warm and costs about $25/month. You need [X] credits at $0.10 each to run the signal plays that are generating your pipeline. And [X] seats so everyone who needs access has it. If we cut any of those, we'd be cutting into the results you're generating. Let me put together a proposal that reflects what you actually need, and then we can talk about how to make the price work."

Don't Say This

"I can take some stuff out to bring the price down."- this frames the conversation as a cutting exercise instead of a value exercise.

Why It Works

When every line item has a reason tied to their results, cutting scope feels like cutting results. The price becomes justified, not arbitrary.

Key Rule

Always come in with a justified reason for every single thing you're proposing. No line item should exist without a clear connection to their goals.

Get Information Before You Haggle

Most reps start negotiating the moment price comes up. That's backwards. The first move in any pricing conversation isn't to counter or concede, it's to gather information. Slow down the urgency to haggle, signal that you're worried about getting stuck, and test the waters with a small concession to read their reaction before making any real move.

Say This

Step 1- Slow down: "Before we get into numbers, I want to make sure we're on the same page about what success looks like. Can we take a step back?"

Step 2- Signal concern: "I'm a little worried we could get stuck on price before we've figured out the right scope. I don't want us to negotiate against a number that doesn't reflect what you actually need."

Step 3- Test with a small concession: "What if I could get you an extra month of onboarding support- would that change how you're thinking about this?" (Watch their reaction. If they bite, price isn't really the issue. If they dismiss it, the objection is real.)

Don't Say This

"What number works for you?" / "Let me see what I can do on price."- Both of these skip the information-gathering step entirely. You're negotiating blind, and you'll always leave money on the table.

Why It Works

Negotiation is an information game. The person with the most information wins. By slowing down, you learn what's really driving the pushback- is it budget, is it perceived value, is it a competing priority, is it a power play? Each one requires a completely different response. If you counter before you know, you're guessing.

Key Rule

Never make your second concession until you've understood the reaction to your first. A small test concession tells you everything: if they take it and keep pushing, the issue is price. If they pause, the issue was something else entirely. Read the room before you move.

Let Their Imagination Do the Work

When a customer is stalling or pushing back, the instinct is to tell them what will happen if they don't move forward. But stated consequences feel like threats and trigger defensiveness. Instead, ask a question that makes them imagine the consequences for themselves. Their own imagination is always more persuasive than anything you could say.

Say This

"If we pause on this and pick it back up in six months, what does that look like for your team's pipeline targets in the meantime?"

"What happens to the reps who are currently using Unify if the tool goes away mid-quarter?"

"If you had to go back to the old process tomorrow, what would that cost your team in hours per week?"

Don't Say This

"If you don't renew, your team will lose all their sequences and pipeline will drop."- Stating the consequence feels like a threat. It creates resistance. Asking the question makes them arrive at the same conclusion on their own, which makes it feel like their idea.

Why It Works

Self-generated conclusions are stickier than externally imposed ones. When someone imagines a negative outcome themselves, they feel the weight of it more deeply than if you'd stated it. You're not pressuring them- you're creating space for them to pressure themselves. And they can never push back against their own imagination.

Key Rule

Frame every consequence as a question, never a statement. "What happens if..." is always more powerful than "If you don't, then..." Then be quiet. The silence after the question is where the persuasion happens. Let them sit with it.

Collaborative Negotiation

Most negotiations feel like two people throwing darts at each other- one person names a number, the other counters, back and forth until someone blinks. This is adversarial negotiation and it destroys trust. Instead, coax the customer into a collaborative conversation where you're both solving the same problem: how to get this deal done in a way that works for everyone.

Say This

"I want to find something that works for both of us. Help me understand what a good outcome looks like on your end- not just the number, but the shape of the deal. Is it about total cost? Payment terms? Scope? If I know what matters most, I can get creative on our side."

"Instead of going back and forth on price, can we zoom out and figure out what the ideal package looks like? Then we'll figure out the number together."

Don't Say This

"Here's our best price, take it or leave it." / "We can't go lower than X." Hard lines create hard reactions. The moment you draw a line in the sand, the conversation becomes positional. You're no longer solving a problem together, you're in a standoff.

Why It Works

When people feel like they're on the same side of the table, they give up information they'd never share in an adversarial setup. You learn their real constraints, their real priorities, and their real flexibility. That information is worth more than any discount you could offer.

Key Rule

Use "we" and "together" language throughout the negotiation. "How do we make this work?" positions you as a partner. "What can I do for you?" positions you as a vendor begging for the deal. The frame matters as much as the number.

5

Objection Handling

When they push back — on price, build vs. buy, competitors, or value — these reframe the conversation in your favor.

Build vs. Buy

When a customer says they could build this in-house or cobble together cheaper tools, don't be defensive. Invite them to pressure-test it. Then make the true cost tangible.

Say This

The Hook: "I actually want you to pressure-test that- because when customers map out what they'd actually have to buy, build, and maintain, the picture changes quickly."

The Stack: "To replicate what you have today: a web visitor ID tool, an enrichment tool, a sequencing tool, a deliverability service, and intent signals. Five tools. Then someone to wire them together- CRM sync, dedup logic, routing, bounce monitoring. That's an engineering hire or your [champion] spending their time on integrations instead of campaigns."

The Invisible Work: "We're warming and managing [X] mailboxes ($25/month each, three weeks to warm from scratch), monitoring deliverability daily, staggering sends, catching web visitors and auto-enrolling them, deduplicating across your CRM. Nobody on your team maintains any of that. The day you go DIY, someone has to."

The Close: "You can find a cheaper sequencer. But the sequencer was never the hard part- it's the warmed inboxes, the signals, the enrichment, the CRM sync, the agent qualification, the dedup, and the team helping you optimize all of it."

Don't Say This

"You can't do that" or "That won't work"- being dismissive makes them want to prove you wrong. Invite the comparison. Confidence wins.

Why It Works

You're not arguing. You're helping them do the math. And the math almost never works in DIY's favor once you include the invisible work, the ramp time, and the opportunity cost.

Key Rule

Always have their specific numbers ready- mailbox count, pipeline influenced, positive replies, a named deal. Generic build-vs-buy arguments are forgettable. Their numbers are not.

Handling Competitive Mentions

Don't trash-talk. List what they'd lose, quantify the cost and effort of rebuilding, and make the alternative feel harder than staying.

Say This

"That's fair- [competitor] does some things well. Here's what I'd challenge you to think about: right now you have [X] warmed mailboxes, deliverability managed daily, [X] plays running, everything synced to your CRM, and our team in the trenches with you every week. If you switch, you're rebuilding all of that from scratch with a team that doesn't know your business. What's that transition worth in pipeline you'll miss during the 3-month ramp?"

Don't Say This

"[Competitor] can't do what we do" is vague and defensive. Instead, make the switch feel expensive by listing specific things they'd have to replace.

Why It Works

You're not saying the competitor is bad. You're saying the transition is expensive. That's harder to argue with because it's true.

Key Rule

Quantify the transition cost in their language: "That's 3 months without pipeline from this channel. At your current run rate, that's [$X] in pipeline you'd be giving up."

"Do Credits Roll Over?"

Say no cleanly. Don't immediately offer free credits or extensions. Then redirect into maximizing usage.

Say This

"Credits are active for the license term and don't roll over. But here's the thing, if you're not using all your credits, that usually means there are signals and plays we haven't turned on yet. Let's look at your usage and figure out where the upside is before the renewal."

Don't Say This

"Let me see if I can get you a credit extension."- you've caved before you even explored why they're under-using. Solve the usage problem first.

Why It Works

You've said no and immediately redirected to a conversation about getting them more value. The question shifts from "Can I get more for free?" to "How do I get more out of what I have?"

Key Rule

If they're genuinely under-using and there's no upside to unlock, right-size the package on the next contract rather than rolling over unused credits.

Reframing "Low Positive Replies"

Positive reply count alone will always look small. Pipeline influenced is the number that justifies the spend. Reframe Unify as an outbound engine, not a reply counter.

Say This

"Positive replies are one output, but they're not the full picture. Those 26 replies? That's 26 meetings your team didn't have to source. But beyond that, you influenced [$X] in pipeline because people who received your emails went to your website, talked to colleagues, and inbounded. The emails are the catalyst, not just the conversion mechanism."

Don't Say This

"26 positive replies is actually a strong number."- if it doesn't feel strong to them, arguing the number is a losing battle. Reframe the metric entirely.

Why It Works

You've shifted the measurement from a metric they find underwhelming to one that justifies the investment. Pipeline influenced is the number their leadership cares about.

Key Rule

Always have the pipeline influenced number ready before any value conversation. Pull it the day before. If you show up without it, you're negotiating blind.

When There's a Blanket Budget Cut

If the CFO says "cut 15% across the board," your job is to help your champion argue that Unify is the exception, because cutting it costs more than keeping it.

Say This

"I totally understand the mandate. Let me help you make the case for why this shouldn't be in the 15%. If you cut Unify, you're cutting the tool that generated [$X] in pipeline this year. Your CFO isn't trying to cut revenue-generating tools, they're trying to cut waste. Let me build you a one-pager that makes that case. What does your CFO care about most- pipeline, cost per meeting, or revenue attribution?"

Don't Say This

"I understand, let me see what discount I can offer."- you've accepted the frame that you're part of the cut. Fight the frame, not the price.

Why It Works

You're not negotiating with the mandate, you're helping your champion circumvent it with evidence. Every CFO has exceptions. Make Unify the exception.

Key Rule

Build the deliverable and send it to your champion 48 hours before their internal meeting. Don't offer to help, just build it and send it.

Interrupt the "Not Right Now"

When a customer starts building the case for "not right now," they're talking themselves out of buying in real time. Every word they say strengthens their own argument. Most reps sit quietly and let this happen, then try to counter after the customer has fully convinced themselves. The move is to interrupt the slide before it builds momentum- respectfully, but firmly.

Say This

"Can I pause you there for a second? I want to make sure I understand what's behind the timing concern, because I'm hearing you say the value is there- is that right?"

"Before we go down the timing path, can we separate timing from value? If timing weren't a factor, would you move forward?"

"I hear you on timing. Let me ask it differently: what changes between now and six months from now that makes this a better decision then?"

Don't Say This

"I totally understand, let's circle back in Q3."- You've just let them off the hook with zero friction. The deal is now dead and neither of you acknowledged it. "Not right now" almost always means "not ever" unless you interrupt the pattern.

Why It Works

People convince themselves through their own words. The longer they talk about why "not now" makes sense, the more committed they become to that position. Interrupting early gives you a chance to redirect while they're still persuadable. You're not being pushy; you're being precise about what's actually driving the hesitation.

Key Rule

Always separate timing from value. If the value is there but the timing isn't, you have something to work with. If the value isn't there, no amount of timing pressure will help. Ask the separating question early: "If timing weren't a factor, would you move forward?" Their answer tells you everything.

"What If We Do This?"

Five words that can redirect any stalled conversation. When things get stuck (on price, on scope, on timing, on anything) instead of arguing against their position, offer an alternative path. "What if we do this?" shifts the energy from confrontation to exploration. It signals flexibility without conceding, and it invites them to collaborate instead of resist.

Say This

"What if we do this- instead of the full platform right now, we start with the core you need this quarter and build in an expansion trigger at the 90-day mark?"

"What if we do this- I lock in this year's rate for a two-year term, and we revisit scope at the midpoint?"

"What if we do this- I extend your current plan by 30 days so your team can finish the evaluation without pressure?"

Don't Say This

"That's not going to work on our end." / "We can't do that." Hard rejections end conversations. Even if you can't do what they're asking, "What if we do this?" keeps the door open and positions you as someone who finds solutions, not someone who says no.

Why It Works

This phrase works because it does three things simultaneously: it acknowledges the impasse (you're not ignoring the problem), it offers a creative path forward (you're being solution-oriented), and it's tentative enough ("what if") that it doesn't feel like pressure. It moves the conversation from "no" to "maybe", and "maybe" is where deals get done.

Key Rule

Always have two or three "what if" options prepared before any negotiation. You should never be improvising alternatives on the spot. Think through the likely sticking points in advance and pre-build creative solutions for each one. The best negotiators don't react, they redirect.

6

Creating Urgency & Momentum

Agreement in principle is half the battle. These tactics create real time pressure and prevent deals from stalling.

Reverse-Engineer the Timeline from Activation, Not the Renewal Date

Don't anchor urgency to the contract date- that's your problem, not theirs. Anchor it to what they care about: getting live, hitting volume, and generating pipeline in Year 2. Work backwards from the activation goal.

Say This

"Here's what I'm thinking about timing. Your goal for Year 2 is to be running 10,000 emails/month across the new SDR team. To get there, we need to onboard the new reps (2 weeks), warm the mailboxes (3 weeks), and build the new plays (1-2 weeks). That means we need to kick off Year 2 by July 1st to be at full volume by August. Working backwards, that means getting the contract wrapped by mid-June so we're not losing weeks. Does that timeline track with what you're seeing on your side?"

Don't Say This

"We need to get this signed by end of month for our quarter." your quota is not their problem. Frame urgency around their outcomes, not your calendar.

Why It Works

When the urgency is about them missing pipeline, not you missing quota, it feels genuine. They're not doing you a favor by signing early, they're protecting their own ramp.

Key Rule

Use specific Unify numbers in the timeline: mailboxes take 3 weeks to warm, onboarding takes 2-4 weeks, play building takes 1-2 weeks. These aren't arbitrary, they're real constraints that create real urgency.

Being the Squeaky Wheel

At the legal/procurement stage, deals stall because people are lazy, forgetful, and procrastinating. A shocking amount of progress comes from simply catalyzing action. You become the squeaky wheel for the champion. The champion becomes the squeaky wheel for legal.

Say This

"[Name]- made a note to check in and see if you heard anything from your legal team on the final review? I'm about to ping my team to make sure we can provide quick answers to any final questions. Have your folks said anything? I'll keep you posted on what I hear on my end."

Don't Say This

"Just checking in!" / "Any updates?" these are easy to ignore. A specific, helpful check-in that shows you're also working on your side is much harder to brush off.

Why It Works

The tone is friendly, helpful, collaborative. It would be irrational for a buyer to get upset about it. Remember: the customer wants to buy your product. You're helping them get there faster.

Key Rule

Don't be afraid of "pushing too hard." If you reframe it- you're helping them get what they want faster- there's no such thing as too helpful.

Book the Next Meeting Live

Non-negotiable. Every call ends with the next meeting confirmed before hanging up. Calendars open, invite sent, confirmed. A deal without a next meeting is a deal that's stalling.

Say This

"Before we wrap, let's get the next check-in on the calendar now so we don't lose momentum. I've got availability [two specific times]. Which works better for your team?"

Don't Say This

"I'll Slack you to find time." / "Let's find time next week."- these never happen. Book it now or it drifts.

Why It Works

A booked meeting creates a forcing function. Both sides have a date to deliver against. Without it, the deal enters limbo.

Key Rule

Offer two specific options, not an open question. "Tuesday at 2 or Thursday at 10?" closes faster than "When are you free?"

Early Renewal Incentives

Deadlines create urgency. Your customer knows this is a sales tactic- that's fine. What matters is whether the incentive is real and the reason is credible. Lean into it honestly.

Say This

"If we can get this wrapped by end of month, I can lock in your current rate card for the next two years. After that, the new pricing goes into effect for everyone. I'm not going to pretend this isn't partly about my timeline too- but the savings are real, and locking in the rate protects you from increases down the road."

Don't Say This

Don't create fake urgency. "My manager said this deal has to close by Friday" feels manufactured. Tie the incentive to something real- rate card changes, credit pricing, a board meeting to show momentum, capacity planning.

Why It Works

Frame the offer so not accepting it feels like a loss. "I can hold this rate until the 30th" = after the 30th, they're losing the discount. People are 2-3x more motivated to avoid losses than to achieve gains.

Key Rule

Be honest about what's driving the deadline. Customers respect transparency. They know you have a quota. Pretending otherwise insults their intelligence.

The Renewal Starts the Day After Signature

The renewal conversation should never feel like a separate topic tacked on at the end. From the day after they sign, every QBR, every check-in, every working session should reinforce why continuing the partnership is the obvious choice. Document results early. Remind them of progress. Surface switching risk casually. By the time the renewal conversation happens, the answer should already be yes.

Say This (mid-contract)

"I know we're not near the renewal yet, but I always want to make sure we're tracking toward making that an easy conversation. Based on what we've built so far, here's where I see the value story shaping up..."

Don't Say This

Don't wait until 90 days before renewal to start talking about value. By then, competitors have already filled the messaging void with their most disruptive pitches.

Why It Works

If you leave a "messaging void" (a period where you provide no strategic value communication) competitors fill it. The renewal conversation should feel like a formality because the value case has been building for months.

Key Rule

Early in the contract: document results, reinforce the decision. Mid-contract: introduce expansion ideas while safely within the term. Pre-renewal: pure reinforcement. Don't introduce disruption close to a decision point.

Move the Invite, Don't Ask Permission

When a customer declines a meeting, most reps send a message asking "when works better for you?"- and then wait. And wait. The meeting never gets rebooked because asking for permission to reschedule gives them an easy out. Instead, just move the invite. Pick a new time, send the updated calendar invite, and add a brief note. The default becomes "we're still meeting" rather than "maybe we'll meet someday."

Say This

(Calendar invite moved to new time, with this note:)
"Saw the conflict- moved us to Thursday at 2pm. I've got a couple things I want to walk you through on [specific topic]. Let me know if the new time doesn't work."

Don't Say This

"Hey, I saw you declined, totally understand! When would be a better time to reconnect?" This is passive, and it gives them infinite room to deprioritize you. You've handed them the power to decide whether the meeting happens at all, rather than just when.

Why It Works

It leverages default bias- people are more likely to accept something that's already on their calendar than to actively schedule something new. Moving the invite also signals confidence: you believe this meeting is worth having, and you're making it easy for them. Asking permission signals uncertainty and makes them question whether the meeting matters.

Key Rule

Always include a reason in the moved invite. "I've got recommendations on X" or "Want to walk through the results from last month" gives them a reason to keep it. A naked calendar invite with no context is easy to decline again. And never move it more than twice- if they decline the moved invite, pick up the phone.

7

Closing & Getting Commitment

Getting from "sounds good" to "signed" requires specific moves.

Getting to a Conditional "Yes"

A conditional yes is better than a maybe. Once you have "yes, if..." you know exactly what to solve. The negotiation shifts from "should we renew?" to "how do we make this work?"

Say This

"It sounds like we're aligned on the value and the direction. If I can get the pricing to [X], are we good to move forward? What else would need to be true?"

Don't Say This

"Let me know what you think" — too open-ended. Push for a conditional commitment so you know exactly what to solve next.

Why It Works

Each condition you solve makes it harder for them to add new ones. You're building momentum toward signature, one solved problem at a time.

Key Rule

Write down their conditions. Solve them one by one. Send confirmation after each one: "Got it — I've confirmed [X]. That leaves [Y] and [Z]." Make the path to signature visible.

"Yes" Without "How" Is Meaningless

Get them to agree to the same thing three different ways in one conversation. The first "yes" might be counterfeit. The second is confirmation. The third is real commitment. Use calibrated questions to get there.

Say This

Agreement 1: "So we're aligned on $48K annual at the Growth tier?" → "Yes."
Agreement 2: "And your team is planning to have this signed by the 15th?" → "That's the plan."
Agreement 3: "How do you want to handle routing the paperwork through procurement?" → (them describing specific steps = real commitment)

Don't Say This

Don't take the first "yes" and move on. One "yes" is cheap. Three "yeses" — especially when the third requires them to describe HOW — is a real commitment.

Why It Works

When someone describes the implementation steps themselves, they've mentally committed. It's much harder to back out of something you've already planned in detail.

Key Rule

The third agreement should always be a "How" question. How will you route the contract? How should we handle the transition? This forces them to plan execution, which locks commitment.

Confirming Every Term Live

Before the deal moves to paperwork, verbally confirm every element. Ambiguity at this stage creates last-minute surprises that reopen the negotiation.

Say This

"Let me make sure we're 100% on the same page before I send the order form. You're looking at: [X] seats, [X] mailboxes, [X] credits, at [$Y/year], on a [Z]-year term starting [date]. [Name] will be the signer. Does that all match what you have?"

Don't Say This

Don't skip this step because it "feels redundant." One misalignment on seats, credits, or start date can delay signature by weeks.

Why It Works

Hearing every term read back forces them to flag any misalignment now, not after the contract is drafted. It also signals professionalism and thoroughness.

Key Rule

Read every element. Price, seats, mailboxes, credits, contract length, start date, signer. If it's in the contract, confirm it verbally first.

Planting Expansion Seeds at Close

The moment after a close is the highest-trust, lowest-resistance point. Use it to naturally plant ideas about future growth without pushing.

Say This

"Really excited about Year 2. One thing I'd love to explore down the road — as your SDR team scales, there's a natural fit to get them into Unify as well. We don't need to figure that out now, but it's worked really well for accounts like yours. I'll keep it on my radar."

Don't Say This

Don't hard-sell expansion at the close. The deal just got done — let them feel good about it. Plant the seed and water it later.

Why It Works

They're feeling positive and committed. An idea planted now sits in the back of their mind and grows naturally. When you bring it up in 90 days, it feels familiar, not new.

Key Rule

Wait until you've delivered demonstrable value before pitching the expansion. Earn the right to sell the next thing. The path to SDRs is through the champion's success.

8

Service Recovery

When things go wrong, research shows customers can think MORE highly of you after a well-handled recovery than if nothing went wrong. But the order matters enormously.

The Five-Step Recovery Framework

Researchers tested 500 participants on different orderings of apology components. One ordering won across every single metric — willingness to buy again, likelihood of buying more, willingness to recommend, credibility, and effectiveness. The "just the facts" approach finished dead last. Here's the exact order that won:

Step 1 — Lead with the Fix

"First — I've gotten approval to extend your contract by one month at no cost, and I've directed our team to manually verify every affected record. You'll have a full report by Friday."

The customer doesn't care about the "why" until they know you're fixing it. Starting with a long explanation while they're fuming makes them MORE frustrated.

Step 2 — Take Full Ownership

"This was entirely on us. It shouldn't have happened, full stop."

No deflecting, no blaming, no hedging. "Our third-party vendor had an issue" is still your problem.

Step 3 — Promise Change

"We've already made changes to our monitoring to make sure this specific failure can't happen again."

Describe what you're changing, not just that you're sorry.

Step 4 — NOW Explain What Happened

"What happened was [specific explanation]. Our backup system didn't catch it because [reason]."

They're calm enough now to absorb it. If you'd led with this, they'd have been too angry to listen.

Step 5 — End with Sincere Emotion

"I'm personally sorry this happened. I know how much work your team has put into this, and the last thing I want is for our mistake to undermine that."

People remember the first and last things most vividly. Fix first (captures peak attention) + regret last (sticks in memory) = optimal emotional arc.

Don't Say This

Don't lead with the explanation: "So what happened was..." — responding to an emotional situation with a rational explanation backfires every time. Fix first, explain later.

Why It Works

This is the Service Recovery Paradox: customers can think MORE highly of you after a well-handled failure than if nothing went wrong. But only if you execute in the right order. Lower the temperature first (the fix), end with emotional resonance (the regret).

Key Rule

Act immediately. Speed matters. Delayed recovery kills trust even if the eventual response is perfect. And always offer compensation before being asked — don't make them fight for it.

Bad News? Pick Up the Phone.

When you get bad news — a deal going sideways, a champion going dark, a renewal at risk — the instinct is to craft a careful email. Don't. Pick up the phone immediately and get curious. Email gives them time to build walls. A phone call catches them in the honest moment before their defenses go up. Your goal isn't to fix it on the call — it's to understand what actually happened.

Say This

"Hey, I just saw [the news/the update/the email]. I'm not calling to push back — I'm calling because I want to understand. Can you walk me through what changed?"

"I'd rather hear it from you directly than guess. What's going on?"

"I know things shifted. I'm not going to try to save anything on this call — I just want to understand where you're at so I can figure out if there's anything I can do."

Don't Say This

Don't send a long email re-pitching the value. Don't respond with a "just wanted to check in" text. Don't wait three days hoping it resolves itself. Every hour you spend crafting the perfect written response is an hour the customer spends hardening their position.

Why It Works

Phone calls create human connection that text can't replicate. When someone hears genuine curiosity in your voice — not panic, not a pitch — they open up. You learn the real reason, not the polished version. And sometimes the real reason is fixable. Deals that look dead in email come back to life on the phone because the customer tells you the one thing they wouldn't have written down.

Key Rule

Lead with curiosity, not with a save attempt. "Help me understand" is the most powerful phrase you can use. If you call trying to fix it, they'll feel sold to. If you call trying to understand, they'll feel heard. Understanding comes first. The fix comes from what you learn.

9

Psychological Foundations

The mental models behind every tactic. Understanding these lets you improvise when no script fits.

Loss Aversion

People hate losing 2x more than they enjoy gaining. This is the most powerful motivator in human decision-making. In every negotiation, frame what the customer stands to LOSE by not acting, not what they stand to gain. "You'll miss $500K in pipeline during the transition" hits harder than "You'll generate $500K in pipeline with us."

Key Rule

Reframe every value statement through the loss lens. Instead of "Here's what you'll get," say "Here's what you'd be giving up."

Status Quo Bias

Four forces keep people chained to their current state: they've made decisions that became preferences, switching feels expensive, similar options cause paralysis, and they fear being wrong. As the incumbent vendor, all four work in your favor. The biggest mistake: using disruptive "new idea" messaging with existing customers makes them 10-16% more likely to switch.

Key Rule

You ARE the status quo. Reinforce it, don't disrupt it. Save provocative messaging for new logos.

Anchoring

The brain anchors to the first number it hears and judges everything relative to it. This works even when people know they're being anchored. Application: set the anchor high before offering your real number. Works on price, contract length, seat counts, and timeline.

Key Rule

Whoever sets the first number controls the negotiation. If the customer anchors first ("We can only do $30K"), reframe before accepting their anchor: "Let me show you why $30K doesn't get you the coverage you need."

Voice & Tone

Your voice is your most powerful tool. Master three modes:

  • Calm & Steady: Deep, soft, slow. Downward inflection. Use for tough pricing conversations and pushback moments. Signals "I'm in control."
  • Positive & Playful: Light, encouraging. Your default for most interactions. Smile while you talk — even on the phone, it changes your tone. People in a positive frame collaborate more readily.
  • Direct: Use sparingly. Plain, simple, friendly, non-negotiable. For declarations: "Credits don't roll over." No explanation, no alternative. Done.
Key Rule

When negotiations get tense, most people speed up. Do the opposite. Slow down. Lower your voice. Calm your pace. When you slow the process down, you calm it down.

The Detective Mindset

Mechanics run the same playbook every time. Detectives build a case — assembling information, addressing every concern, making the argument easy for the jury (your customer's boss) to follow. The $180K rep runs the same motion on every deal. The $400K rep adapts based on context. Be the detective. Be curious. Build the case. Make it easy for the room you're not in to say yes.

Key Rule

Before every call, ask yourself: "What do I not yet know about this account that could change how I approach this conversation?" If you can't answer, you haven't done enough discovery.

Repetition Is Persuasion

The most important things you say should be the things you say most often. If your key message — the ROI, the outcome, the core value — only shows up once in a conversation or once in a deal cycle, it won't stick. People don't remember what they hear once. They remember what they hear repeatedly, in different contexts, from different angles. Repetition isn't redundancy — it's reinforcement.

Say This

On the call: "The core result your team is seeing is a 40% increase in qualified pipeline."

In the follow-up email: "As we discussed, the 40% pipeline lift is the headline number for your leadership."

In the QBR deck: "This quarter's theme: 40% more qualified pipeline, driven by..."

In the champion prep: "When your boss asks 'why Unify,' the answer is: 40% more qualified pipeline."

Don't Say This

Don't introduce a new "most important thing" every conversation. If you mentioned pipeline lift last call and switch to time savings this call and switch to deliverability next call, nothing sticks. Your champion can't sell what they can't remember, and they can't remember what you only said once.

Why It Works

Cognitive psychology calls this the "mere exposure effect" — repeated exposure to a message increases familiarity, and familiarity increases trust and agreement. In sales, this means your core value prop needs to show up in every touchpoint: calls, emails, decks, Slack messages, QBRs. Not word-for-word repetition, but the same core idea delivered in contextually relevant ways. By the time the renewal conversation happens, the value case should feel like a known fact, not a new argument.

Key Rule

Pick your one or two "headline metrics" per account and weave them into every single touchpoint. Different words, same message. The goal is that when your champion walks into the internal meeting, they can recite the value case from memory — because you've said it enough times that it's their language now, not yours.

Quick Reference

10 Cardinal Rules

  • Never offer a discount before making them reveal their number.
  • Never give anything without getting something back (Give-Get).
  • Always lead with ROI before discussing price.
  • Always book the next meeting live on the call.
  • Lead with the fix when things go wrong, not the explanation.
  • Use "you" language — the customer is the hero.
  • Mirror their last words and shut up. Silence is the weapon.
  • Frame everything as what they'll lose, not what they'll gain.
  • Design questions for "No" to get to "Yes."
  • Slow down. Speed kills trust and deals.
  • Get information before you haggle — never counter blind.
  • Earn the right to every meeting. If you can't say why it's worth 30 minutes of their day, cancel it.
  • Repeat your core message at every touchpoint. Repetition is persuasion.

Conversation Selection

  • Same solution, renewing → Reinforce status quo. Document results, remind them of their original decision, mention switching risk, highlight cost of change, show you're keeping pace.
  • Price increase → Same as above + anchor high, offer loyalty discount down.
  • Upsell / expansion → Reinforce first, then introduce evolving pressures as natural progression. Share "hard truths" about where current solution falls short. Paint the upside.
  • Service failure → Fix → Own → Promise → Explain → Apologize. That exact order. If it's bad news, pick up the phone — don't hide behind email.
  • New division / new buyer → Treat like an acquisition sale. You're not the incumbent there.
  • Customer going dark → Move the invite (don't ask to reschedule). If declined twice, pick up the phone and get curious.
  • Champion selling internally → Run the 5 Questions, then build them a one-pager 48 hours before their internal meeting.
  • Price pushback mid-deal → Slow down. Pour cold water, test with a small concession, read the reaction. Then go collaborative — "help me understand the shape of a good deal for you."

Things That Always Backfire

  • Using provocative "new idea" messaging with existing customers (makes them 10-16% more likely to switch).
  • Leading with product features in an upsell (lost across the board in research).
  • Using "just the facts" in an apology (consistently finished last).
  • Citing "external cost factors" as the reason for a price increase.
  • "We-phrasing" instead of "You-phrasing" (loses 9-21% across key metrics).
  • Waiting until contract end to start value messaging.
  • Offering discounts proactively before the customer has asked.
  • Pitching workflows to decision makers (they buy outcomes) or pitching outcomes to practitioners (they need the workflow).
  • Stating consequences as threats — ask the question and let their imagination do the work.
  • Letting a customer talk themselves into "not right now" without interrupting the slide.
  • Sending a long email when you get bad news instead of picking up the phone immediately.
  • Saying your core value prop once and assuming it stuck. If you only said it once, nobody remembers it.
